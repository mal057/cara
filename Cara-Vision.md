# Part One: The Story

## The Moment Everything Changed

There was a week in the summer of 2022 when millions of women in America opened their phones and did something that would have seemed absurd a month earlier: they deleted their period trackers.

Not because the apps stopped working. Not because they found something better. They deleted them because they were afraid. The Supreme Court had overturned *Roe v. Wade* with the Dobbs decision, and overnight, the most intimate data a woman could generate — the dates of her cycles, the days she felt nauseous, the weeks she skipped — became potential evidence. Prosecutors in states with abortion bans could, in theory, subpoena that data. The apps women had trusted with the rhythm of their bodies were suddenly a liability.

And so the apps were deleted. Millions of them. Quietly. In bedrooms and bathrooms and parked cars. Women lost something small but real that day — a little ritual of self-knowledge, a daily habit of paying attention to their own bodies. The data was gone, and with it, the sense that technology was on their side.

That loss is where Cara begins.

## Why Cara Exists

Cara exists because trust, once broken, has to be rebuilt with architecture, not promises.

Every period tracker on the market, even the ones that responded to the post-Dobbs panic with updated privacy policies and reassuring blog posts, still operates on the same fundamental model: your data leaves your device. It goes to a server. It sits in a database. That database is owned by a company. That company is subject to laws, subpoenas, data breaches, acquisitions, and the ordinary greed that turns user data into revenue. No matter how sincere the privacy policy, the structure is the same. Your body's data is someone else's asset.

Cara rejects that structure entirely.

There is no server. There is no database in a data center somewhere. There is no account creation — no email address, no phone number, no password, no identity verification of any kind. When you open Cara for the first time, there is no login screen. There is no onboarding flow that asks for your name. There is just the app, ready to be yours.

Every piece of data you enter into Cara stays on your device. It is stored in local storage that belongs to you, on hardware that belongs to you. There is no sync. There is no cloud backup. There is no "share with your partner" feature. There is no export function that could be compelled. If you lose your phone, you lose your data — and that is a feature, not a flaw. It means no one else can ever have it either.

You could hand your unlocked phone to a prosecutor, and what would they find? An app. With data on it. Data that never went anywhere, was never transmitted, was never stored on any system they could subpoena independently. The privacy is not a policy you have to trust. It is the architecture itself. Cara cannot betray you because it literally does not have the capability.

This is what it means to build technology that serves instead of surveils.

## Who Cara Is For

Cara is for every woman who deleted her period app in the summer of 2022 and felt that small, strange grief.

It is for the woman who wants to track her cycle — to know when she is ovulating, to anticipate the days she will feel tired or tender or electric — without handing that knowledge to a corporation. It is for the woman who has been pregnant and wants to understand her body's return to rhythm. It is for the woman who has never been pregnant and wants to understand her body, period. It is for the teenager whose cycles are still irregular and who is learning, for the first time, to pay attention to the patterns. It is for the woman approaching menopause who wants to notice the changes with clarity instead of confusion.

It is for women who are tired of the transaction. Tired of the implicit deal that says: you can understand your body, but only if you let us understand it too. Only if you give us your email. Only if you accept the cookies. Only if you let us build a profile. Only if you become the product.

Cara asks for nothing. It gives.

## The Name

Cara means "beloved" in Italian. It means "dear one" in Irish. It is not clinical. It is not a brand-agency creation designed to test well in focus groups. It is what you would call someone you are taking care of.

The name sets the tone for everything. When you open Cara, you are not opening a medical device or a productivity tool or a wellness platform. You are opening something that was made with affection. Something that treats your attention as precious and your data as sacred. The name is a promise in a single word: *you are dear to us, and we will act like it.*

## What Makes Cara Different

The period tracker market is crowded. Flo, Clue, Glow, Ovia, Natural Cycles — they all do roughly the same thing, and they all make roughly the same promise: we take your privacy seriously. But "taking privacy seriously" in the context of a client-server architecture is like saying you take fire safety seriously while building with wood. You can mitigate the risk. You cannot eliminate it.

Cara eliminates it.

The distinction is not "we don't sell your data." The distinction is that there is no data to sell. There is no data to breach. There is no data to subpoena. There is no data to hand over to an acquiring company with different values. There is no data to leak in a security incident. The data exists in exactly one place — your phone — and when you delete the app, it is gone.

This is not a privacy policy. It is a privacy *architecture*. And the difference matters. Policies can change. Policies are written by lawyers and revised by executives and overridden by courts. Architecture is physics. If the data never leaves the device, no policy change, no executive decision, no court order can make it appear on a server where it never existed.

Cara is also different in what it refuses to do. There is no social layer. There is no community forum. There is no "share your cycle with your partner" feature, because your cycle is yours. There are no ads — not for prenatal vitamins, not for tampons, not for fertility clinics. There is no premium tier. There is no upsell. The app is complete as it is, from the first moment you open it.

## The Philosophy

There is a way of building technology that treats people as resources to be extracted. Their attention is extracted for ad revenue. Their data is extracted for profiling. Their habits are extracted for behavioral prediction. Their insecurities are extracted for upsell opportunities. This is the dominant model. It is so pervasive that we have stopped noticing it, the way you stop noticing the hum of an air conditioner.

Cara is built on a different premise: technology should serve. It should make your life slightly better without making someone else's life slightly worse. It should give more than it takes. It should respect the person using it as a full human being, not a collection of data points to be monetized.

This is not naivete. It is a choice. Building Cara means choosing craft over commerce. It means building something beautiful and useful and giving it away, not because there is a business model hidden underneath, but because the act of building it is the point. Because care is its own reason.

And there is something specific about who is building this. Cara is being built by a Black man. There is no savior narrative here — this is not about rescuing anyone. It is about solidarity. It is about understanding that when systems of power decide to surveil and control bodies, the response is to build systems that protect them. Black people in America have a long and intimate understanding of what it means to have your body be someone else's concern, someone else's data point, someone else's property. Building Cara is an extension of that understanding. It is saying: I know what it means to not be safe, and I can build something that makes you safer.

Care is an act of resistance in an extractive world. Cara is care made into software.

## The Emotional Core

Imagine this moment:

You pick up your phone in the morning. You open an app. There is no login screen. There is no loading spinner while it syncs with a server. There is no notification asking you to rate the app or invite a friend. There is just a clean, calm interface. Soft colors. Clear typography. A gentle visualization of your cycle. You tap today's date. You note how you feel. You close the app.

The whole interaction takes twelve seconds. And in those twelve seconds, something important happened: you paid attention to yourself. You checked in with your body. And the record of that check-in did not go anywhere. It did not become a data point in an advertising profile. It did not get stored on a server in Virginia. It did not become discoverable evidence. It stayed with you. Yours. Private. Safe.

That feeling — the feeling of something being simply, completely yours in a world that is constantly asking you to share, to connect, to sync, to upload — that feeling is the product. Not the features. Not the algorithm. Not the prediction engine. The feeling of safety. The feeling of something being made for you, with no ulterior motive.

That is what Cara gives. And it is enough.

## The Bigger Picture

Cara is small. It is one app, on one phone, tracking one cycle. It will not change legislation. It will not dismantle surveillance capitalism. It will not solve the crisis of trust between technology and the people it was supposed to serve.

But it is a proof of concept for a different way of building. It demonstrates that you can make something useful and beautiful without extracting anything from the person who uses it. It demonstrates that privacy can be an architectural guarantee, not a legal negotiation. It demonstrates that technology can be an act of care.

And maybe that is enough, too. Maybe the revolution is not one big thing but a thousand small ones — a thousand quiet decisions to build something that gives instead of takes. Cara is one of those decisions. Made with love. Made with intention. Made because someone should, and because we could.

*Cara. Beloved. Dear one. Yours.*

### Scene 1: The Deletion

**Prompt:**
Extreme close-up of a woman's hands holding a smartphone, finger pressing and holding on a period tracking app icon, the familiar iOS jiggle-delete animation beginning. The screen casts a cold blue-white glow upward onto her face, which is partially visible — just her chin, lips pressed together, the lower curve of a worried eye. Background is a dim bedroom, out of focus, warm amber tones from a distant bedside lamp contrasting with the sterile phone light. Shallow depth of field. The phone screen shows the app's red delete confirmation dialog. Her fingernails are unpainted. The mood is quiet dread. Cinematic 35mm film grain. Color palette: cold steel blue from the screen, warm amber in the background, muted skin tones. Aspect ratio 16:9.

**Tone:** Intimate loss — a private moment of surrender disguised as a practical decision.

### Scene 2: The Headlines

**Prompt:**

Jackson," "Digital Surveillance of Women's Health," "Your Cycle Data Is Not Safe," "Delete Your Period App." Headlines rendered in sharp white and red sans-serif type, stacking and dissolving, some highlighted in yellow marker strokes.
